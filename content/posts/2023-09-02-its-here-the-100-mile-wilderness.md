There is a sign at the southern end of the 100-Mile Wilderness that has been photographed by every thru-hiker for the last fifty years. It reads, more or less: *Do not enter this section without at least ten days' food supply. There are no roads for resupply. You are on your own.*

When you read that sign after walking from Georgia — after Pennsylvania's rocks, the heat of Virginia in summer, the exposed ridges of New Hampshire — it lands differently than it would at the start. You are not scared. You are ready.

## What the 100-Mile Wilderness actually is

It's not quite 100 miles. It's closer to 99, depending on who's measuring. It runs from Monson, Maine — a small town best known to hikers as the last resupply point — to Baxter State Park at the base of Katahdin. Between those two points, there are no towns, no paved roads, and very limited cell service.

The terrain is not the hardest on the trail. After the White Mountains, nothing east of the Mississippi feels quite as demanding. But the remoteness is real. If something goes wrong out here — a bad fall, a medical issue — help is genuinely far away.

## The shelter system

Maine's shelter system is excellent. The lean-tos are maintained by the Maine Appalachian Trail Club and are reliably spaced at reasonable distances. In September, after the peak summer crowds have thinned, you may have entire shelters to yourself. I did, more than once, and found it one of the quietest stretches of the entire trail.

## What I remember most

Rain. More rain than I had experienced since Pennsylvania in June. The kind of steady, cold Maine autumn rain that soaks through everything eventually and requires a proper commitment to forward motion to stay warm.

And then, on the fourth day, a clear morning. I climbed White Cap Mountain in the early light and saw, for the first time, Katahdin — the end — rising above the treeline to the north. Still a long way off. Still real.

> The mountain looked the way that endings look when you finally believe in them.

## Practical notes for hikers

**Food:** Ten days is the recommendation. Eight is realistic for a fit thru-hiker in September. Do not cut this close. There are hostels at the midpoint area (around Shaw's and White House Landing) if you pre-arrange a resupply drop, but they are not guaranteed.

**Water:** Plentiful. Maine has almost too much of it. Standard filtering protocols apply.

**Timing:** September is the sweet spot — bugs are gone, crowds have thinned, the maples are starting to turn. The risk is weather. October is beautiful and much colder; early November is wilderness survival territory.

**The final approach:** Baxter State Park has strict rules about camping and permits, and Katahdin itself has a daily summit limit. If you are thru-hiking, you are typically given latitude, but check current rules before you go. The summit closes in October.
