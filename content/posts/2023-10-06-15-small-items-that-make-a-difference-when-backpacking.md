After completing the Appalachian Trail, I have strong opinions about gear — mostly about what I wish I'd brought sooner. The big items (tent, pack, sleeping bag) get all the coverage. This list is about the small things: the items that cost under $30, weigh almost nothing, and quietly improved daily life on trail in ways I did not anticipate.

These are not affiliate recommendations made for clicks. These are things I actually carried and would carry again.

---

## 1. Micro-fibre camp towel (30cm × 60cm)

I resisted carrying a towel for the first three weeks. I was wrong. Even a very small one — the kind that dries in two hours — transforms post-swim camp stops, hostel showers, and unexpected rain events. Get one small enough that you forget it's in your pack.

## 2. Ear plugs

Shelters. Hostels. Other people's snoring. Enough said. Foam ear plugs weigh nothing and cost essentially nothing and I slept better for the second half of my hike than the first because of them.

## 3. Trowel with depth gauge

Catholes done correctly require 6–8 inches of depth. A trowel with a gauge removes the guesswork. The Leave No Trace rules exist for good reasons, and this is a small way of taking them seriously.

## 4. Waterproof stuff sacks (dry bags)

Not your whole pack — just your sleeping bag, your down layers, and your electronics. Even in a waterproof pack, sustained rain finds its way in eventually. Protecting the things that cannot be wet is worth the 40 grams.

## 5. Lip balm with SPF 30+

Sunburned lips are one of the most avoidable miseries on trail and one of the most common. I went through four of these on the AT and would do so again.

## 6. Electrolyte tablets (not powder)

Powder clumps. Tablets don't. On hot days with high mileage, water alone is not enough — the cramping is real and it starts in your calves first. I used one tablet per litre in the afternoons.

## 7. A small sewing kit

Needles, thread, two safety pins. The entire thing fits in a film canister. In five months on trail I used it for: a blown seam in my rain jacket, a strap on my pack that came loose, a rip in my sleeping bag stuff sack, and someone else's emergency gear repair. It weighs essentially nothing.

## 8. Gaiters (trail gaiters, not mountaineering gaiters)

Low trail gaiters keep debris out of your shoes. On rocky, leaf-covered trail in autumn, this is the difference between stopping every 45 minutes to remove a pebble and just walking. I dismissed these as unnecessary until mile 400. I have not dismissed them since.

## 9. Microspikes

Only relevant for shoulder-season hikers, but deeply relevant then. I carried mine for the last 300 miles of Maine in late September and used them twice. The security on icy granite slabs is not nothing.

## 10. Silk liner for your sleeping bag

Adds 5–10°F of warmth for very little weight and volume. Also keeps your sleeping bag cleaner for longer, which matters when you're washing your gear infrequently. I used mine from Katahdin south when the nights dropped.

## 11. Trekking pole tips (spare rubber tips)

The carbide tips that come standard on trekking poles are fine for dirt and rock. The rubber caps that fit over them are essential for hardwood floors in hostels, metal surfaces on bridges, and general courtesy in shelters. They are also better on wet rock. Carry two spare pairs.

## 12. A headlamp with a red light mode

Red light preserves night vision and doesn't wake other shelter occupants when you're up at 5am. Most headlamps have it. Make sure yours does and remember it's there.

## 13. Foot powder

Between toes, specifically. Athlete's foot on trail is a genuine problem and a frustrating one to fix in the middle of a long stretch. Prevention costs 30 grams and a few seconds each morning.

## 14. A small notebook and pen

I used my phone for journalling until it died in a thunderstorm in Pennsylvania. After that I used a 40-page Rite in the Rain notebook and a Fisher Space Pen. Both work in the rain. Both worked for the rest of the trail. The notes from those months are the thing I'm most glad I have.

## 15. Emergency contacts on a waterproof card

Your phone can die, get lost, or get wet. A laminated card with emergency contacts, your blood type, and any relevant medical information costs nothing and lives in the hip belt of your pack where someone else can find it. I have never needed mine. I am glad it's always there.
