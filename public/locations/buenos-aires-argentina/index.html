South America · Argentina

Buenos Aires, Argentina

Mar – Apr 2023

Buenos Aires is one of those cities that takes you completely by surprise. I arrived expecting a stopover and ended up staying six weeks. There is something about the pace of the place — late dinners, afternoon coffee that stretches into evening, Sunday markets that don’t get going until noon — that makes the rest of the world feel slightly frantic by comparison. The city is enormous (population 3 million in the city proper, 15 million in Greater BA) and deeply neighbourhoood-defined. Palermo is not San Telmo is not La Boca. Getting comfortable in one corner takes a week; understanding the whole city takes much longer. I’d recommend staying at least two weeks if you can.

Do
San Telmo Market (Feria de San Telmo)
Sundays only — antiques, street performers, empanadas, and some of the best people-watching in the city. Go early, leave late.
Do
La Boca & Caminito
Colourful and touristy, but worth an hour. Walk in, see the painted houses and tango dancers, then eat at Parrilla El Obrero nearby — it's where the locals actually go.
Do
Recoleta Cemetery
One of the most beautiful cemeteries in the world. Free entry, genuinely atmospheric, and home to Eva Perón's tomb. Budget at least an hour.
Do
Palermo Soho & Hollywood
The best neighbourhood for walking, café-hopping, and vintage shopping. The streets are lined with jacaranda trees that bloom purple in November.
Eat
El Preferido de Palermo
Classic bodegón — the kind of old-school Argentine lunch spot that's been there forever. Get the milanesa and the house pasta. Cash only, book ahead.
Eat
Las Cabras
Enormous, cheap, and delicious empanadas on Fitz Roy in Palermo. Always a queue — it moves fast. Order a dozen.
Eat
Don Julio (Palermo)
The best parrilla experience in the city if you're splurging. Reserve a table, order the bife de chorizo, and do not skip the provoleta.
Stay
Palermo Soho or Palermo Hollywood
The safest, most walkable, and most interesting neighbourhood to base yourself. Lots of good mid-range Airbnbs. Avoid staying in Microcentro unless you're there for work.
Tip
Get a Sube card immediately
The public transit card for Buenos Aires — covers all buses and the subte (metro). Available at kioscos (corner shops) throughout the city. Cash is king for smaller purchases.
Tip
Learn a few words of Rioplatense Spanish
Argentine Spanish is distinct — 'vos' instead of 'tú', 'sh' sound for 'll' and 'y'. Locals appreciate any effort. Even 'che, boludo' will earn you laughs if used correctly.
